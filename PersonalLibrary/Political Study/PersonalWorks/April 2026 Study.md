## [The Secret Beginnings of the Bolsheviks: From Reading Circles to Revolution](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=we-sodFPJVI) 

### Notes

- "In late 1893, the 23-year-old Vladimir Ilyich Ulyanov, better known as Lenin, arrived in St. Petersburg after being exiled for student political activity. At the time, the city's Marxists were divided into smaller, uncoordinated circles. When he established contact with one of them, he noticed that these collectives lacked connections to real political struggles. In that same year, he presented his work on the so-called market question, using Marxist economic analysis to show how capitalism was developing naturally in Russia, refuting the analysis by the then dominant Narodism, a form of utopian socialism... With his ability to link theoretical with important practical questions, he impressed one of the bigger Marxist study groups. He especially made an impression on Nadia Krupskaya, a young woman from an impoverished noble family, who later wrote that his approach represented living Marxism, connecting theory to real social processes."
	- When Ilyich arrived in St. Petersburg, he arrived with some prior experience in the Marxist methodology and into a milieu of young Marxist organizing.
	- His understanding and practical use of Dialectical Materialism allowed him to help these young formations to approach material problems through the correct theoretical and practical means and impressed upon them his faithful understanding and application of Marxism.

#### Introduction to Socialism

> "Socialism is a transitionary period between capitalism and communism. Historically, this transition period has only occurred in war-torn countries that were either freshly out of feudalism or semi-feudal and had just thrown off their colonial shackles... They had to figure out how to both develop their productive forces and redistribute land without going into another civil war and set up socialist institutions in the process. That's pretty damn difficult. Because every society's starting point on the socialist journey is different, there's no one size fits all definition of what socialism looks like."

- The road ahead will be difficult to navigate, and a fine balance must be kept between attaining progress and maintaining socialist construction.

> "Because every society's starting point on the socialist journey is different, there's no one size fits all definition of what socialism looks like. In most cases, we're not even looking for a straight line where you constantly have less and less capitalism and more communism. There are times in which you genuinely have to take one step back, so you can then take two steps forward. But of course at the same time, there are markers and baselines we can set. If not, we could call anything socialism!
> 
> We're essentially looking for a 'general direction'. Do policies as a totality move society towards communism, or back towards capitalism? What's the vision for where the country needs to go? And what's the plan for getting there? The ultraleft position on socialism says: 'Phase out commodity production, meaning production for profit instead of need, right away and don't you dare look at material conditions. It's time to go go go! This line leads to commandism, which is a type of leadership where you force people to do things they don't want. The type of leadership we're looking for is the mass line. 
> 
> This means you take people's current concerns, short term goals, and put it in a revolutionary framework, the long term goal of liberation."

- The road ahead is not straight; there will be detours and collisions and the like to delay us. But we must not race ahead and leave the people behind! By holding formation and leading well, the whole convoy will make it safe and sound.

> "Phasing out commodity production unfortunately isn't everyone's immediate concerns. So if you'd push this onto people, it would lead to resentment and take away support for the revolution. The ultraright position says that socialism is all about developing productive forces.
> 
> Because of this overfocus, unnecessary concessions are made. Why? Because capitalism tends to develop productive forces quicker because it does so unevenly. This would lead to either the development of capitalist social relations, or the return to them. Of course if there are unnecessary concessions, that means that there are necessary concessions. The red line poses that developing the productive forces is important, but that politics should always be in command. And that even when you have concessions you need to make, you need to be able to explain why and how this will strengthen the revolution in the long run other than just saying: don't worry about it, it's always good to develop productive forces."

- We must not get distracted by shiny products of development which are markedly uneven. As much as the current material conditions allow, all growth in the productive forces ought to be led politically and by the masses of the workers rather than a small clique of developers who will forget about rural populations. 
	- I believe this particular point to be especially true in our country, highlighted by the recent insufficiencies and closures of rural healthcare facilities due to funding losses.

> "(In this video, we'll find out that) developing the relations of production (for example, more equality) will actually unleash higher productive forces (for example, all the infrastructure built during the Great Leap Forward)."

- This idea is even more important to grasp within the imperial core nations, such as the US. Our advanced productive forces are some of the strongest in the world, but our basic productive forces, our manufacturing and processing and weaving facilities, for some examples, have been gutted over time to make space in the domestic market for cheaper imported products. 
	- More study in to this ought to be done, but my immediate thoughts on this point are twofold:
		- 1: If we wish to regain our proletarian self-sufficiency, to free ourselves from the political-economic yoke of the international bourgeois foodways and manufacturing monopolies, we must reinvent our basic productive forces.
		- 2: The act of reinventing our basic productive forces, specifically if done through development of our relations of production, would constitute a heavy blow against the productive monopoly of capital. 
			- This task can be undertaken by the entirety of the proletariat local to any region, as a wide variety of basic production is needed to fuel any sort of advanced production.

#### China's Starting Point 
##### Land Reform

> "...in 1946 the Statistical Bureau of the Guomindang, which was the government at the time, stated that China had only 8% of its total land under cultivation. And this was actually shrinking because of erosion, mismanagement, and other environmental reasons. Not a great place to start off a revolution in which you're trying to feed everybody. As should come as no surprise, landlords were an enormous parasite on the Chinese economy. Over 50% of all crops went to landlords for rent. More than half of Chinese farmers were tenants. What made things even more complicated was that most peasants saw their landlords as untouchable. So much so that it was hard for cadres to convince peasants to act against their landlords, even while the CCP was in power! So of course it makes sense that the starting point of the revolution would be land reform. The pace of the land reform movement differed per part of the country and in how far and deep it went... the country was facing uneven development in reaching its revolutionary goals. But one thing would remain the same for all campaigns to come: the masses had to be on the front lines and enact the campaign themselves. This meant they had to be mobilized, and then encouraged to act."

- In pre-revolutionary China, the vast majority of the production of the basic necessities of human life went to benefit a very small minority class. In modern US, the vast majority of the products of the alienated human labor which acts as a spectaclist stand-in for the basic necessities of human life are going to benefit a very small minority class. 
	- Qualitatively, the situation is similar; but there is a great quantitative divide, driven deeper every passing year by the ideological wedge of the Spectacle,

- Mao was born on December 26th, 1893 in Hunan Province, in the village of Shaoshan
	- Shaoshan was the ancestral home of the Mao clan; as a result, nearly everyone there was related and had the same family name Mao.
- Shaoshan was in a relatively prosperous farming region, and his father made enough through hard work to expand the family farm and hire a couple of laborers.
	- According to the criteria that Mao laid out in "How to Differentiate Class Status in Rural Areas", his family would have been classified as a rich peasant family.

> [!quote] "The rich peasant as a rule owns land. But some rich peasants own only part of their land and rent the remainder. Others have no land of their own at all and rent all their land. The rich peasant generally has rather more and better instruments of production and more liquid capital than the average and engages in labor himself, but always relies on exploitation for part or even the major part of his income. His main form of exploitation is the hiring of labor (long-term laborers). In addition, he may let part of his land and practice exploitation through land rent, or may lend money or engage in industry and commerce. Most rich peasants also engage in the administration of communal land. A person who owns a fair amount of good land, farms some of it himself without hiring labor, but exploits other peasants by means of land rent, loan interests or in other ways, shall also be treated as a rich peasant. Rich peasants regularly practice exploitation and many derive most of their income from this source."
